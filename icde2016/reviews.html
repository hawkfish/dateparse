Title Does Anybody Really Know What Time It Is? Automating the Extraction of Date Scalars
Masked Reviewer ID: Assigned_Reviewer_1
Review:
Question 
Overall Rating Reject
Strong Points
S1: Hard but important problem
S2: Thorough solutions
Weak Points
W1: Weak problem description / problem investigation
W2: Presentation is not as is expected of a research paper
W3: Unclarities in the experimental section
Detailed Comments
The paper is about extracting date values from columns that hold a date
in string form. The paper presents two approaches (MDL and NLP) that each
perfomed above 95% and agreed with each other quite substantially.

The problem of dealing with date values of varying formats is a hard and
important problem (S1). The motivation of its importance is okay. Both
algorithms are iquite elaborate and capable of recognizing and processing
a wide variety of date formats (S2).

W1: the description and investigation of the problem is weak. The motivation
and description of the problem remain on high abstraction level, but there is
no exact definition of the problem in terms of "Given ..., we attempt to
produce ...". Therefore, it remains rather unclear until far in the paper that
your work starts from columns of date values (i.e., not date values embedded
within plain text), what typical hard problems are, what assumptions you make
(e.g., that all values in the column are/are not expected to be of in the
same format), etc. Also the motivation refers to uncited studies ("we see
users performing" ... who is we?; "our visualization system"; "our studies"):
are these scientific studies with a rigorous and systematic research method?
Such unverifiable studies are not solid enough for providing evidence about
the importance of the problem (although I'm personally convinced of this as
well). Some important remarks are in Section VI on related work, that should
be in the introduction.

W2: The presentation of the technical part of the paper feels more like
software documentation than a research paper. A research paper should
describe a generic technique (that is preferably broader applicable than
your application) on a well-chosen level of abstraction. Some but certainly
not all of the details given here are scientifically relevant. Mathematical
notation would have been helpful here to give a more concise and exact
presentation of the problem and both of your solutions. Especially the MDL
technique is described in such a way that I ask myself: is there some
insight to be gained here or is it just that we have a new algorithm?

W3: You pay quite some attention to the experimental set up, but still
some things are unclear. The test data is taken from "data sets published to
a free, online data analysis website" ... but not which one! How can this
research be repeated to verify your results and claims? Furthermore, how
is the golden truth determined, which results do you consider wrong and
which not? My guess is that you only looked at those results for which both
solutions disagreed, but this doesn't account for cases where they are
both wrong (you should at least mention this). Finally, why don't you use
the commonly accepted metrics Precision and Recall here?

Other remarks:
III: The MDL approach is based on Potter's Wheel algorithm which apparently
employs structure enumeration. Because of its importance, a summary of the
these techniques is desirable, especially if you are also making remarks
about its computational expensiveness (III.C). Without such a summary, a
general reader cannot verify your claims.o

The second approach is named "Natural Language Processing". I find it an
ill-chosen name, because it has little to do with common approaches in
NLP. Calling it a PCFG approach or simply "(probabilistic) grammar approach"
would better fit, IMO.

There is an awkward leftover comment to "vidya" on page 8.

Page 9: there is a reference to Table 2 containing summary statistics, but
this is an erroneous reference.

Wise choice to leave out date values that Excel touched. These are indeed
very dangerous. You may be interested in reading this one:
B.R. Zeeberg, J. Riss, D.W. Kane, K.J. Bussey, E. Uchio, W.M. Linehan,
J.C. Barrett, and J.N. Weinstein. Mistaken identifiers: gene name errors
can be introduced inadvertently when using excel in bioinformatics.BMC bioinformatics, 5(1):80, 2004.

Review:
Question 
Overall Rating Reject
Strong Points - Practical problem that is important is nicely motivated
- Two different algorithms are provided and cross-validated
Weak Points - Limited research contribution (both algorithms are extensions of previous techniques)
- The level of detail used throughout the paper is fluctuating; some pieces of detail are provided repeatedly.
- The claim of 95% accuracy (mentioned in the abstract and contribution sections) is not backed by the evaluation results
- A detailed description of how the ground truth was created and used in the evaluation is missing
- A detailed comparison of MDL and PCFG is missing (I would love to see one table summarizing the main differences between both results sets)
- The data used for the evaluation is not public
Detailed Comments The authors present two algorithms for automatically parsing dates written as columns of strings, one based on minimal entropy representation and another one based on natural language processing techniques.

The two techniques described in this paper are both pragmatic and intuitive. They are both however relatively simple extensions of existing techniques; the first technique extends Minimum Descriptive Length [9-10]. The second one uses probabilistic context-free grammar, an existing parser, and supervised learning.

The evaluation section is in my opinion not convincing. First, the claim of 95% accuracy (made in the abstract and contribution section) is not backed up by the empirical results. The authors should include a number of baselines comparing the effectiveness and efficiency of their approach to existing techniques. Also, they should describe in more detail the how they built and used the dataset, and should if possible make the data available.

- Section V, B, 1) Reference to wrong table
- Section V, B, 1) Mention of “left hand side” in Figure 3 unclear
- Section V, B, 2) Last sentence is not clear, missing parentheses.

Question 
Overall Rating Neutral
Strong Points Very useful in practice and would be a nice addition for relational
DBMS, not just visualization systems.

Nice and simple algorithms, concisely described.
Weak Points Evaluation could be better.

Does not handle columns with differing date formats.
Detailed Comments The paper presents two algorithms for automatically detecting the date
format of a set of strings. The algorithms seem sensible.

The authors should compare their error rates with the built-in
functions (in particular of Postgres), since these have heuristics for
detecting date and time formats, too. These should serve as a natural
baseline. Also, the evaluation does not conclude which of the two
algorithms is better in the end or which Tableau would use. What are
the true/false positive rates for both approaches?

The paper would be stronger if the authors also addressed the case
where the same column has multiple differing date format. The kind of
data sets where automatic date format detection is useful are often
combined from different sources. Also, a purely local approach based
on casting at runtime might actually be much better unless multiple
differing date formats are explicitly handled.

Please provide a link to the "free, online data analysis website".

The remark at the end of section 4 should be removed.
